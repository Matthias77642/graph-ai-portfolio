Data Analyst Job Market Report

Introduction

In this assignment, we used Large Language Models (LLMs) directly within Google Sheets to automatically extract structured information from approximately 400 data analysis job postings. The dataset originates from a Glassdoor project designed to analyze the Data Scientist, Data Engineer, and Data Analyst job market in June 2020. Glassdoor is an American technology and career information platform best known for allowing employees and job seekers to anonymously review companies, share salary information, and search for job opportunities. By combining features of a job search engine with a workplace review site, Glassdoor aims to help individuals make more informed career decisions. Using LLM-powered prompts embedded in Google Sheets, we extracted two key pieces of information from each job posting: the number of years of professional experience required and the programming language requirements. Specifically, we identified whether each position required proficiency in R, Python, both languages, or neither. This approach demonstrates how LLMs can be integrated into spreadsheet-based workflows to efficiently transform unstructured job descriptions into analyzable, structured data.

The dataset contains 400 job postings. After removing rows with missing salary or experience data, 309 postings are available for the experience-vs-salary analysis. The median salary estimate is $68,000 and the median minimum years of experience requested is 3 years.

Interpretation

There is no convincing evidence for a relationship between years of experience and salary in this dataset. Although a weak upward trend can be observed in some parts of the distribution, the overall pattern is highly dispersed. Job postings with similar experience requirements often report a wide range of salary estimates, indicating that experience alone is not a reliable predictor of compensation. In particular, positions requiring relatively few years of experience sometimes offer salaries comparable to, or even higher than, those requiring substantially more experience. This lack of a clear relationship suggests that other factors play a more prominent role in determining salary levels. Differences in job title, industry, employer type, geographic location, and technical skill requirements likely outweigh the effect of experience as captured by minimum years requested in job postings. The salary-by-data-language analysis indicates meaningful differences in compensation based on programming language requirements. Positions requiring Python tend to have higher median salaries than those requiring R only or neither language. Roles that require both R and Python appear among the highest-paying postings, suggesting that employers place a premium on candidates with broader technical skill sets and flexibility across data science workflows. Jobs that do not explicitly require either language generally cluster at lower salary levels. This may reflect roles with a stronger focus on reporting, business intelligence, or non-programming analytical tasks, which often command lower compensation than more technically intensive data science or engineering roles.
