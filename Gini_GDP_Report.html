Gini Coefficient and Income per Person – Global Report (2020)

Introduction

This report examines two key development indicators for the year 2020:

  1. Gini Coefficient – a measure of income inequality within a country, where 0 represents perfect equality and 100 represents perfect inequality.
  2. Income per Person (GDP per capita, PPP in 2021 US dollars) – a measure of economic output per person adjusted for purchasing power parity.

The data is sourced from the Gapminder dataset.

The scatterplot below examines the relationship between the Gini coefficient and income per person across 191 countries.

Correlation Analysis

The Pearson correlation coefficient between the Gini coefficient and income per person is r = -0.279 (p = 9.25e-05), and the Spearman rank correlation is rho = -0.342 (p = 1.26e-06).

Interpretation

The scatterplot reveals a weak negative relationship between income inequality (Gini coefficient) and income per person. Countries with higher incomes per person tend to have slightly lower Gini coefficients, suggesting that wealthier nations are somewhat more equal in their income distributions. However, the relationship is far from deterministic, and there is considerable scatter around any trend line.

Several notable patterns emerge:

  • High-income, low-inequality countries: Many European nations (e.g., Denmark, Finland, Sweden, Belgium, Czech Republic, Slovenia) cluster in the lower-right area of the plot – they combine high incomes with relatively low inequality.

  • High-inequality countries: South Africa (63), Suriname, Botswana, and Namibia stand out with very high Gini coefficients (above 55), indicating severe income disparities regardless of their income levels.

  • Middle-income, high-inequality countries: Several Latin American countries (Brazil, Colombia, Panama, Honduras) show moderate income levels but notably high inequality.

  • Low-income countries: Many Sub-Saharan African and South Asian countries have relatively low incomes per person, but their inequality levels vary widely – from fairly equal (e.g., Ukraine, Moldova) to highly unequal (e.g., Central African Republic, Mozambique).

The LOESS smoother (blue curve) suggests the relationship is not purely linear: at very low income levels, inequality varies widely, while at higher income levels there appears to be a modest downward trend in inequality. This is broadly consistent with aspects of the Kuznets curve hypothesis, which posits that inequality first rises and then falls as economies develop, though the cross-sectional data shown here captures only a snapshot at one point in time.

Overall, while income level provides some information about a country’s inequality, many other factors – including historical, institutional, and policy-related differences – play crucial roles in determining income distribution within a country.
